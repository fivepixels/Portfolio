How I Got Here

Migration, reinvention, and the human side of my creative practice.

Migration & Reinvention

My path into design and technology was not linear. I came to the United States looking for better opportunities, and that decision forced me to rebuild my life through many kinds of work, from fabrication shops and coffee bars to school programs, public performance, and creative technology.

I do not see that background as separate from my design practice. It taught me to adapt quickly, work with limited resources, communicate across cultures, and stay curious in unfamiliar environments. That perspective shapes the way I build interactive experiences today.

Barista

Before working in AR, I spent time roasting and serving Ecuadorian coffee in Quito. It was a deeply human job built around attention: listening to people, reading the room, creating small moments of comfort, and understanding how environment changes behavior.

That experience still influences how I design. Good interaction design is not only about interfaces. It is about timing, atmosphere, empathy, and making people feel invited into an experience.

Street Performer

“Just before the sun rises the sky is at its blackest”

- Thomas Fuller

Starting over required resilience, humor, and the ability to keep moving through uncertainty. Performing in public taught me how people respond to energy, timing, vulnerability, and presence.

That lesson still matters in my work. Whether I am designing for a phone, an AR mirror, or a stadium screen, I am always thinking about attention, participation, and the moment when someone decides to engage.

Industrial Design Roots

Before leaving Venezuela, I studied Industrial Design at the University of Los Andes. My coursework introduced me to design studios, research methods, form theory, fabrication, ergonomics, prototyping, and visual communication.

Even though my path changed, that foundation never left. It gave me a physical, material understanding of design before I moved into digital tools, AR, and interactive systems.

Learning Through Movement

Travel shaped the way I understand people. In my younger years, I spent time camping, climbing, hitchhiking, and moving through different communities across Latin America, including cities, rural towns, the Andes, and parts of the Amazon region.

Those experiences made me more observant and open-minded. They taught me to listen before assuming, to adapt to different contexts, and to connect with people whose lives looked very different from mine. That curiosity is still part of how I approach design.
